Here's an overview of our project.

This project aims to investigate the level of engagement that tweets spreading misinformation or disinformation about the alleged sale of Scarborough Shoal by Noynoy Aquino and Trillanes generate, compared to other factual tweets on the same topic.

Motivation

The motivation for this project is the increasing prevalence of misinformation/disinformation on social media platforms such as Twitter and its potential negative impact on public discourse and decision-making. By studying the engagement patterns of false tweets on a specific topic, this project can shed light on how misinformation/disinformation spreads on social media and how it can be effectively addressed through fact-checking and other interventions.

Problem

The problem in question for this project is to investigate the level of engagement that tweets spreading misinformation or disinformation regarding the story about Noynoy Aquino and Trillanes selling Scarborough Shoal receive on Twitter. The project aims to explore the different types of engagements, such as likes, replies, retweets, and quote retweets, and how they change over time, with a focus on important events such as news coverage and the release of fact-checking articles.

Solution

The ultimate goal is to understand how misinformation and disinformation spread on social media and how it affects user engagement. This information can be helpful for social media companies, policymakers, and other stakeholders who are concerned about the impact of misinformation/disinformation on public opinion and democratic processes.

Here are the project's objectives.

Research Question

How much engagement does a tweet spread misinformation/disinformation regarding the story about Noynoy Aquino and Trillanes selling Scarborough Shoal garner? How did this change over time?

Hypothesis 1

Tweets spreading misinformation/disinformation regarding the story about Noynoy Aquino and Trillanes selling Scarborough Shoal are likely to have more quote retweets and replies than likes and retweets.

Hypothesis 2

The number of engagements (i.e., likes, replies, retweets, and quote retweets) on tweets spreading misinformation/disinformation also dropped over time after the release of fact-checking articles.

Null Hypothesis

There is no significant difference in the type of engagement (quote retweets, replies, likes, and retweets) received by tweets spreading misinformation/disinformation regarding the story about Noynoy Aquino and Trillanes selling Scarborough Shoal, and there is no significant change in the number of engagements over time after the release of fact-checking articles.

Action Plan

Analyze false tweets ot tweets containing misinformation and look at their engagements over time and see how they changed while also taking note of important events such as news coverage and the release of fact-checking articles.

General Processing

  • Handling missing values/ensuring no missing values

    • Removed the following columns with no entries for all observations/rows: Tweet Translated, Screenshot, Views, and Rating

    • Removed empty rows starting at row 152 and onwards

    • Based on the research question, hypotheses, and problem analysis approach, and considering the context of the dataset, the fields that are relevant for the analysis are as follows:

      • ID: This field can be useful for identifying and referencing specific tweets.

      • Tweet URL: This field can access the tweet content and extract information for analysis.

      • Account handle: This field can help identify and analyze tweets from specific accounts or users.

      • Account name: This field provides additional information about the account associated with each tweet.

      • Account type: This field indicates whether the account is identified or anonymous. It can be useful for differentiating between different types of accounts in the analysis.

      • Tweet: The actual content of the tweet is essential for understanding and analyzing the spread of misinformation/disinformation.

      • Tweet Type: This field may provide information about the type of tweet, which can be useful for filtering and analysis.

      • Date posted: This field, along with the Timestamp field, can be used for analyzing the temporal aspects of engagement and changes over time.

      • Likes: This field is relevant for analyzing the engagement metric of likes, which is mentioned in Hypotheses 1 and 2.

      • Replies: This field is relevant for analyzing the engagement metric of replies mentioned in Hypotheses 1 and 2.

      • Retweets: This field is relevant for analyzing the engagement metric of retweets, which is mentioned in Hypotheses 1 and 2.

      • Quote Tweets: This field is relevant for analyzing the engagement metric of quote retweets, which is mentioned in Hypotheses 1 and 2.

    • The remaining fields (Timestamp, Group, Collector, Category, Topic, Keywords, Account bio, Joined, Following, Followers, Location, Content type, Reasoning, and Remarks) may not directly relate to the research question, hypotheses, or problem analysis approach described.

  • Handling outliers

    • Calculate the z-scores for the following fields: [Likes, Replies, Retweets, Quote Tweets]
    • Identify the rows with z-scores greater than or equal to 3 or less than or equal to -3
    • Drop the rows with the identified outliers

  • Ensuring formatting consistency(date, labels, etc.)

    • Ensured that dates were of the form datetime to ensure consistency. Additionally, given that one of the hypothesis requires the checking of changes of engagements after the release of fact-checking articles there is a need to sort the dataset based on the value of the Date posted value of each observation.

  • Normalization/Standardization/Scaling

    • Z-score standardization can be applied to the numerical engagement variables (Likes, Replies, Retweets, Quote Tweets) in the dataset. Here's why it can be useful:

      • Comparison across Variables: Z-score standardization transforms the variables to have zero mean and unit variance, which allows for a fair comparison across different engagement metrics. By standardizing the variables, you remove the influence of different scales and magnitudes, enabling a meaningful comparison of the relative engagement levels between different types of tweets.
      • Hypothesis Testing: Z-score standardization can help in testing the hypotheses. For example, Hypothesis 1 suggests that tweets spreading misinformation/disinformation are likely to have more quote retweets and replies than likes and retweets. By standardizing the engagement variables, you can compare the standardized scores and assess whether the difference in engagement levels between these types of tweets is statistically significant.
      • Data Visualization: Standardizing the engagement variables allows you to visualize and interpret the distribution of engagement levels easily. You can plot standardized scores on a common scale, making it easier to identify patterns or outliers in the data.
      • Statistical Analysis: Z-score standardization is useful when applying certain statistical techniques that assume variables are normally distributed and have comparable scales. By standardizing the engagement variables, you can meet these assumptions and enhance the validity of the statistical analysis.

    • With this in mind we will add four new fields to the dataset to store the z-scores for the engagement metrics, and this fields will be the following:

      • Likes_zscore
      • Replies_zscore
      • Retweets_zscore
      • Quote Tweets_zscore

  • Categorical data encoding

    • The only field with a categorical value in our dataset is the Account type hence we will make use of One-Hot Encoding wherein we create binary columns for each category, representing the presence or absence of that category. Additionally, One-Hot Encoding is suitable for nominal data with no inherent ordering which is what Account type is.
